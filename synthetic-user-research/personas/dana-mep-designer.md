# Persona Card — Dana Liu, PE

**Role:** Senior mechanical engineer at a 200-engineer ME consulting firm
**Firm type and size:** Mid-size MEP consultancy. Mixed market: offices ~40%, labs ~30%, healthcare ~20%, education ~10%.
**Tenure / certifications:** 12 years. PE-licensed in NY, NJ, CT. ASHRAE member. LEED AP BD+C.
**Geography:** Northeast US.

## Standards literacy

- **ASHRAE Handbook — Fundamentals, Ch. 21 (Duct Design).** Equal-friction sizing at ~0.08–0.10" w.g./100 ft target. Static-regain method for medium-pressure mains. Aware of T-method.
- **ASHRAE 62.1 (Ventilation for Acceptable Indoor Air Quality).** Outdoor-air calc, multi-zone recirculation.
- **ASHRAE 90.1 (Energy Standard).** Duct leakage compliance, insulation requirements (Table 6.8.2).
- **ASHRAE 55 (Thermal Comfort).** Supply-air temperature ranges.
- **SMACNA HVAC Duct Construction Standards.** Knows pressure class designations (½", 1", 2", 3", 4", 6", 10" w.g.) and uses them in the duct schedule. Defers to the contractor on gauge selection — does not read SMACNA gauge tables herself in daily work.
- **IMC, IECC** for code paths.
- **ASHRAE Guideline 0** — Cx process. Knows what the Cx agent will ask of her at design review.

## Daily tools

- **Trane TRACE 3D Plus** (or Carrier HAP) — load and energy modeling.
- **Revit MEP** — primary authoring tool. Uses MagiCAD or built-in duct router for sizing.
- **AutoCAD MEP** — legacy projects.
- **Bluebeam Studio** — drawing review, markup, RFI response.
- **Procore** — RFI / submittal portal on awarded projects.
- **BIM 360 / Autodesk Construction Cloud** — federated model collaboration.
- **Microsoft Project** — schedules.

## Daily workflow

Manages 3–5 active projects in parallel. Each project moves through schematic design → design development → construction documents (where the duct schedule and pressure-class decisions get committed) → construction administration. Stamps drawings. Reviews shop drawings and submittals during CA. Responds to RFIs (~5–15 mech RFIs per major project, of which 1–3 are duct-construction-related). Issues clarification bulletins when RFI volume on a question gets high. Spends roughly 60% of time in design, 25% in CA, 15% in QA / coordination meetings.

## Where the proposed product touches her work

She *creates* the artifact. Pressure class flows from her design into (a) the duct schedule, (b) the legend note, (c) the spec (Section 23 31 13). She is *not* a primary user of an extraction tool — she's the upstream actor. But she eats RFI volume from downstream misreads, so she has skin in the game.

## Initial pain framing (researcher hypothesis)

- PDFs strip Revit parameters. Downstream consumers can't access her structured model.
- Pressure-class-related RFIs cluster around system transitions (main → branch, upstream → downstream of VAV).
- Inconsistency between her schedule, her legend note, and her spec sometimes triggers genuinely valid contractor RFIs — her own coordination errors.
- Shop drawing review takes too long; she'd review more if she could verify dimensions and pressure class without re-keying.

## Buying authority

- Discretionary band: $0 — software at her firm goes through IT and procurement.
- Approval threshold: anything new requires a champion, an evaluation, an IT review. 12–18 month decision cycle.
- Final decision-maker if above threshold: firm CIO + practice leaders (managing principals).

## Notes for embodiment

- More measured than a sub-contractor — she's a stamping engineer; words have liability weight.
- Will distinguish *design* pressure class (what the system needs to operate) from *construction* pressure class (what the duct is built to). They're related but separate.
- Will defer SMACNA gauge specifics ("what gauge is a 36×24 at 2" w.g.?") to the contractor — that's not her layer.
- Knows BIM well but laments the gap between her authored Revit model and what shows up on the PDF deliverable.
- Skeptical of tools that try to replace design judgment; receptive to tools that reduce coordination drag.
